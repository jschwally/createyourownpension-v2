Sequence of Returns Risk: Why Timing Is Everything in Retirement

By John Schwalenberg  |  March 2026

Most retirement conversations focus on average returns. How much did the market return last year? What is the long term average? What rate of return should I assume in my plan?

Those are reasonable questions during accumulation. But retirement changes the math completely.

When you begin taking income from your portfolio, the order of returns matters far more than the average. A bad stretch of market performance in the early years of retirement, while you are making withdrawals, can do lasting damage that no subsequent recovery can fully repair. This is called sequence of returns risk, and it is one of the most consequential and least discussed risks in retirement planning.

The three scenarios below use actual S&P 500 returns from 2000 through 2025, a period that included the Dot Com collapse, the Great Financial Crisis, and Covid, to illustrate exactly how this plays out.

Scenario 1 proves that without withdrawals, the order of returns is completely irrelevant. The same returns in reverse order produce the identical ending balance.

Scenario 2 shows what happens when a $1,000,000 portfolio begins taking $40,000 in annual withdrawals starting in the year 2000, right into one of the worst stretches in modern market history.

Scenario 3 shows how splitting a $600,000 portfolio at age 60, allocating a portion to a Guaranteed Lifetime Withdrawal Benefit annuity and leaving the remainder completely untouched, produces dramatically better outcomes while generating the same $40,000 in annual income starting at age 70.
