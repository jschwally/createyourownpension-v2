Sequence of Returns Risk: Why Timing Is Everything in Retirement

By John Schwalenberg  |  March 2026

Meet Hope and Helen

Hope and Helen are twin sisters. They turned 60 this year. Both have worked hard their entire careers and both have saved $500,000 for retirement. They have the same goal: generate $40,000 a year in retirement income starting at age 70 and make their money last as long as they do.

Their strategies could not be more different.

Hope is confident in the market. She keeps her full $500,000 invested and lets it grow for 10 years. By age 70 her portfolio has dropped to $454,800 after weathering two major market crashes. She begins withdrawing $40,000 a year and trusts that the market will continue to recover.

Helen is a planner. At age 60 she splits her $500,000. She allocates $284,380 to a Pension Strategy and leaves the remaining $215,620 in the market. Helen's investment portfolio has also suffered the same market losses, dropping to just $134,445 at its lowest point in year 3 at age 62. But here is the difference: Helen never needs to touch it. Her pension is funding all of her income. Her portfolio simply recovers and keeps growing uninterrupted. At age 70 her annuity begins paying $40,000 a year, guaranteed for life regardless of what markets do.

Both sisters take $1,000,000 in total income over 25 years. Both experience the same market returns. The difference is how those market returns affect their retirement accounts along the way.

Hope's story: Hope's full market strategy works reasonably well because markets eventually recover. After withdrawing $1,000,000 over 25 years she still has $3,700,315 at age 95. But here is what the ending number does not show: every market downturn for 25 years was a direct threat to her income. In 2008 her portfolio dropped to $359,447 while she was still accumulating. In 2022 it dropped again while she was withdrawing. Hope got lucky. In a different sequence she could have run out of money. She never knew for certain which outcome she would get.

Which Sister Are You?

Hope's strategy worked out. But it required 25 years of uncertainty and a lot of luck with market timing. Helen's pension strategy worked better and she never had a sleepless night about her income. The good news is you do not have to choose between growth and security. You can have both.
