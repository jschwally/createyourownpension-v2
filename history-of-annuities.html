The History of Annuities

By John Schwalenberg  |  June 2025

Annuities can feel like a modern financial product, but the idea is thousands of years old, long before retirement planning looked anything like it does today.

An Ancient Idea

The concept traces back to the Roman Empire, where citizens could make a large payment to the government in exchange for a steady stream of income for life. These payments were called annua, the Latin word for yearly. The basic trade, a lump sum in exchange for predictable income, is the same one behind annuities today.

Annuities in the Middle Ages

By the Middle Ages, similar arrangements had become common across Europe. Governments and churches used them to raise money for public projects. A city might offer citizens regular yearly payments in exchange for an upfront investment, giving investors reliable income during uncertain times while the city funded its projects.

A More Structured Approach

By the 17th and 18th centuries, annuities began to resemble what we recognize today. Governments started using life expectancy data to estimate how long payments would need to continue, which made the arrangements more structured and fair. Early pension systems for soldiers and civil servants were built on this same idea: regular income paid out over time.

Annuities Arrive in America

Annuities became widely available in the United States in the 1800s through insurance companies. Over time they evolved to include features like income guaranteed for life, growth tied to investment performance, and options for leaving remaining funds to a beneficiary.

What This Looks Like in Real Life

A classic example: someone who is 65 with $200,000 saved converts it into an annuity that pays $1,000 a month for the rest of their life. Even if they live into their 90s, the payments continue.

Another common structure is a deferred annuity. Someone invests $50,000 at age 45, lets it grow for 20 years, and then begins income at 65, creating a future paycheck they built years in advance.

It Is Not Just Insurance Products

Annuity-like income shows up in places you might not expect. Social Security works the same basic way: you contribute over your working years, then receive a monthly payment for life in retirement. Lottery winners face a similar choice. Powerball winners, for example, can choose to receive their winnings as annual payments spread over decades instead of one lump sum.

Different Structures, Same Idea

Modern annuities come in several forms. Some tie growth to the performance of a market index. Others include income guarantees that let you withdraw a set amount every year for life, even if the underlying account balance eventually reaches zero.

Through thousands of years and countless variations, the core idea has never really changed. Whether it was a Roman citizen, a medieval investor, or someone building a retirement plan today, the goal is the same: turn savings into a steady, dependable stream of income.
